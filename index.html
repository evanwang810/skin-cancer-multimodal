How the model works

It's a two-tower network. The photo and the patient metadata are read by separate encoders, their outputs are concatenated into one vector, and a small classifier turns that into a single probability of malignancy.

image pathway metadata pathway malignant output benign output

Each node above is a stand-in for a whole layer of real units, not a single neuron — the widths are illustrative. What matters is the shape: two independent pathways that never see each other's raw input, joined only at the fusion step, so the classifier decides using both the pixels and the context at once.

The scatter below plots that fused vector, reduced to two dimensions with PCA (three, in the 3D view), so lesions the model reads as similar sit close together and the benign and malignant groups pull apart. The axes carry no clinical unit — they're just the directions of largest variation in what the model learned. A clean split is a good sign; the cases in the mixed middle are where it's least certain.

FusionThe two vectors are concatenated — no cross-attention, just a joined representation.256 + 32 = 288
ClassifierOne hidden layer with dropout, then a 2-logit head; softmax gives P(malignant).288 → 64 → 2
